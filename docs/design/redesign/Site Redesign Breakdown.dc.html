01 · Diagnosis

Why the site feels clunky

The masthead identity is strong and the data underneath is genuinely deep. The friction is structural: five findings, all fixable with design alone.

1

One identity, two systems

The bold masthead (VOL IV · NO. 27, Anton, the gradient rule) stops at the fold. Below it, every page falls back to stock component defaults — so the product reads generic exactly where the reader spends their time.

2

Law, signal, and analysis all look the same

The platform's core differentiator — verified fact vs. early signal vs. labeled analysis — exists only as italic prefixes buried in prose ("Analytical inference:", "FACT:"). A reader can't tell at a glance whether a paragraph is binding law or an inference.

3

Five pages, five list patterns

Regulations is a 4-column kanban (94 cards, one column 64 deep). Research groups by theme. Operations uses region accordions. Market Intel uses bands. Dashboard uses a feed. Scanning skill learned on one page transfers to none of the others.

4

Internal machinery leaks into the reading surface

Raw markdown pipe-tables render as prose in Sources sections. Scheduling labels ("Always · 10"), gapped section numbers (§4 → §8), schema names in empty states, and triplicate tag chips ("+189 more") all surface to the customer.

5

No wayfinding below page level

Briefs run 15+ sections and thousands of words with no table of contents, no position indicator, no breadcrumb, and no visible expression of how items connect across surfaces. The 7-item sidebar is fine; everything inside a page is unmapped.

03 · Trust labels — the centerpiece

Can I act on this? The label answers first

Every paragraph and row is labeled in plain words before you read it. Verified — confirmed against the official document. Unverified — early word worth watching, not yet confirmed. Our analysis — the platform talking, not the source. Always spelled out, never a code or an icon to memorize. Orange stays out of it (orange only ever means "click here"), and red stays reserved for urgency.

Verified

Confirmed against the official document — a regulation, ruling, or filing. The citation is always attached so you can check it yourself. Blue = on the record.

Unverified

Early word — credible enough to watch, not yet confirmed against a primary source. Amber on a dashed rule: dashed means not yet solid.

Our analysis

The platform's own read — what it implies, what to do about it. Written in ink and always labeled, so our voice can never be mistaken for the law. When a read needs a lawyer's sign-off, the label says so: "Confirm with counsel," in quiet rust.

Severity keeps your gradient scale gradient-bar tokens · systematized

The bar shows how far up the scale an item has climbed — the whole path, ending at its current level. You read severity by where the color stops, not by memorizing four hues. Each stop is a full hue apart: green, yellow, amber, red.

Where it renders: stat-tile underline · ledger band headers · detail hero top edge. The trust label says who is speaking; the gradient bar says how loud. They never use each other's surfaces. The orange→blue brand gradient stays masthead-only.
