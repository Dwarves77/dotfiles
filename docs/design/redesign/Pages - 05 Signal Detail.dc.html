What's moving and what triggered it
Verified · EIA published statistics

WTI spot at Cushing stood at $95.96/barrel on June 1, recovering from an intraday low of $91.16 on May 29. Brent stood at $98.29/barrel, also recovering from $92.88. The April monthly average for Brent was $117.29/barrel, with a spot high of $138/barrel on April 7. EIA Spot Prices (Daily), release 6/3/2026.

Verified · what triggered the spike

The EIA STEO states that Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, the UAE, Qatar, and Bahrain collectively shut in 10.5 million barrels per day in April. Jet fuel (Gulf Coast) stood at $3.569/gallon June 1; the May monthly average was $3.943/gallon. EIA STEO, release May 12, 2026.

Our analysis

The combination of a Strait of Hormuz disruption and a major Gulf producer departing OPEC represents a structural supply-side shock with no single trigger. The partial normalisation now visible in spot prices (Brent recovering to $98 from $138) does not imply a return to pre-April levels in the near term. For air-primary freight operations, the pass-through of jet fuel surcharges lags spot price moves by days to weeks depending on carrier contract terms — creating a margin compression window during rapid price movements in either direction.
Who's driving it and what they want
Verified

The UAE's departure from OPEC, effective May 1, 2026, reduces OPEC's collective spare capacity. The EIA now forecasts OPEC spare capacity averaging 2.5 million b/d in 2027, against a previous forecast of 3.8 million b/d — a reduction of 1.3 million b/d. U.S. crude production averages 13.6 million b/d in 2026, with growth projected to 14.1 million b/d in 2027.

Our analysis

The UAE's exit from OPEC is structurally significant: operating outside the cartel's production framework, UAE spare capacity can no longer be relied on as a rapid-response buffer to stabilise prices in a new disruption event. The cartel's effective response capacity is now narrower. On the demand side, no sourced data identifies a specific buyer constituency driving demand — U.S. output growth is not a near-term price suppressor at current levels.

Conversion triggers · what would shift this path

Hormuz shipping traffic failing to recover to pre-conflict levels by Q3 2026 (the EIA's own stated recovery assumption — if violated, the downward price path stalls or reverses). A second OPEC production event or further member departures reducing cartel coordination. The next STEO lands June 9, 2026 with updated Hormuz recovery data — it may revise the Q2/Q3 forecast.

Inventory context: the EIA forecasts global oil inventories decreasing 2.6 million b/d in 2026 (vs. 0.3 previously — a 2.3 million b/d revision), falling an average 8.5 million b/d in Q2 — limiting downward price pressure even after Hormuz flows rise.
Operational and cost implications by mode
Air — primary mode · highest exposure $3.943 /gal May avg

Monthly path: Mar $3.697 → Apr $3.928 → May $3.943/gal. From the Dec 2025 baseline ($1.965/gal) that is a doubling of Gulf Coast jet fuel cost from late 2025 to spring 2026.

Our analysis · negotiation moment

For verticals where the workspace bears fuel cost risk — time-critical live events, humanitarian, film/TV — the spike and partial recovery create a window where spot-indexed contract terms beat fixed-rate terms. This is a negotiation moment, not a monitoring moment.

Road — secondary mode $3.969 /gal ULSD May

Geographic spread is material: Los Angeles ULSD ran $4.183/gal vs. $3.862 Gulf Coast — a $0.32/gal structural west-coast premium.

Our analysis

Drayage and last-mile for high-value automotive, fine art, and luxury verticals — where white-glove ground transport is non-substitutable — price off ULSD benchmarks. The west-coast premium is structural and should be reflected in quote templates.

Client conversation talking points
What the workspace can credibly say

EIA data confirms jet fuel averaged $3.943/gal in May 2026, up from $1.965 in Dec 2025, driven by the Hormuz closure and production shut-ins — published government statistics, not commercial estimates. The May 12 STEO projects Brent declining to $89/bbl by Q4 2026 and $79 in 2027; a forecast the EIA revises monthly, next on June 9.

Pitfalls to avoid

Do not represent the EIA trajectory as a guarantee of surcharge reduction — the April forecast was significantly revised in May. Do not cite carrier surcharge rates without confirming the carrier's own recalculation cycle; spot price and surcharge are not the same number. Do not claim ocean costs are declining without checking bunker prices separately.

Questions to pose to clients

Are current project budgets indexed to Q4 2025 fuel levels? The gap to May 2026 actuals is ~$2/gal on jet fuel — is a budget revision conversation needed? For live events and film/TV with Q3 2026 schedules: has fuel cost been modelled at EIA-projected Q3 levels (Brent $89–$95 range) or at Q4 2025 lows?
