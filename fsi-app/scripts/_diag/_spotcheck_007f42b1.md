# TxDOT Freight Planning: Statewide Framework and Resources

## Market Signal Brief

---

# Purpose and Scope of This Document

This brief covers TxDOT's active 2027 Texas Freight Mobility Plan (TFMP) update cycle, the Texas Freight Advisory Committee (TxFAC) governance structure, and the federal policy alignment requirements shaping both. It is written for workspaces operating as freight forwarders, importers, and logistics intermediaries active on U.S. domestic and cross-border corridors — particularly those moving cargo through Texas gateways (border crossings, Gulf ports, intermodal hubs) across air, road, and ocean modes.

The brief answers: what is Texas planning for freight, who is shaping those plans, what federal requirements frame them, and what the workspace should do while the planning window is open and stakeholder access is live.

*Source: TxDOT Texas Freight Mobility Plan page, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/projects/planning/freight-planning/texas-freight-mobility-plan.html

---

# What's Moving and What Triggered It — MONITORING

**Severity: MONITORING**

**What is moving:** TxDOT is executing a formal four-year update of its statewide freight plan. The 2027 Texas Freight Mobility Plan (TFMP) update is currently in active development, running from late 2025 through late 2027. *Source: TxDOT Texas Freight Mobility Plan page, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/projects/planning/freight-planning/texas-freight-mobility-plan.html

**What triggered it:** FACT — The 2027 TFMP "will be the four-year update of its 2023 plan, in accordance with requirements in the Fixing America's Surface Transportation Act and 49 USC 70202." *Source: TxDOT Texas Freight Mobility Plan page, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/projects/planning/freight-planning/texas-freight-mobility-plan.html

**Federal alignment layer:** The 2026 National Freight Strategic Plan (NFSP), published by USDOT, "establishes a coordinated national framework for strengthening the safety, efficiency, resilience, security, innovation, and workforce of the U.S. freight system" and "provides clearer planning and analysis frameworks for State and local agencies to consider and build upon during their own freight planning processes." *Source: National Freight Strategic Plan (NFSP), U.S. DOT, updated May 18, 2026. https://www.transportation.gov/freight/NFSP

*Analytical inference:* The 2026 NFSP update — which post-dates the 2023 Texas plan — creates direct alignment pressure on the 2027 TFMP update. TxDOT's 2027 plan will likely incorporate the 2026 NFSP's six strategic goals (safety, efficiency, security, resilience, innovation, workforce) as a structuring framework, given the federal requirement for state plans to align with federal freight policy. Re-check window: mid-2026 when TxDOT begins finalizing recommendations.

**Federal guidance gap:** The Federal Register source at https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/02/24/2026-03648/guidance-on-multimodal-state-freight-plans-and-state-freight-advisory-committees was not accessible from source content (access blocked). The guidance title — "Guidance on Multimodal State Freight Plans and State Freight Advisory Committees," February 24, 2026 — is listed as a corroborating source lead but its specific provisions cannot be cited as FACTs in this brief. *Specific provisions of the February 2026 Federal Register guidance not available from primary sources as of 2026-06-10.*

# Who's Driving It and What They Want

**TxDOT as plan author and convener:**

FACT — The plan "will capture Texas freight transportation assets, conditions, current and future needs, and economic importance through the lens of critical supply chains as well as broader findings related to freight operations and international trade." *Source: TxDOT Texas Freight Mobility Plan page, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/projects/planning/freight-planning/texas-freight-mobility-plan.html

FACT — "The TFMP will culminate in recommendations and strategies including policies, programs, and projects for TxDOT to advance over the next four years as well as in the medium and longer term." *Source: TxDOT Texas Freight Mobility Plan page, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/projects/planning/freight-planning/texas-freight-mobility-plan.html

**Texas Freight Advisory Committee (TxFAC) as private-sector voice:**

FACT — TxFAC's mission is to "Advise TxDOT on freight issues, priorities, projects, and funding needs for freight improvements; and elevate freight transportation as a critical component of the state's economic vitality, and competitiveness." *Source: Texas Freight Advisory Committee, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/about/advisory-committees/texas-freight-advisory-committee.html

FACT — TxFAC goals include: "Ensure the participation of private sector freight stakeholders in TxDOT's transportation planning process," "Provide input into the development of the Statewide Freight Plan," and "Assist TxDOT in prioritizing freight investments by identifying high-priority and strategic freight transportation projects that facilitate the safe and efficient movement of freight throughout the state." *Source: Texas Freight Advisory Committee, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/about/advisory-committees/texas-freight-advisory-committee.html

**Named TxFAC membership (sourced):**

FACT — Current TxFAC membership includes representatives from the following organizations: IBC (Chair: Gerald Schwebel), Port Houston (Candice Armenoff), Owner-Operator Independent Drivers Association – OOIDA (Ken Becker), Office of the Governor (Adriana Cruz), Texas Trucking Association (John Esparza), TNW Corporation (Joey Evans), Southern Dallas County Inland Port Transportation Management Association (Laura Freeland), Mission Economic Development Corporation (Teclo Garcia), Ports to Plains Alliance (Lauren Garduno), Hillwood (Nicholas Konen), TECMA Transportation Services (Miriam Kotkowski), Texas Farm Bureau (Charlie Leal), TEMPO (Robert MacDonald), LDR Partners LP (Ryan Martin), North American Strategy for Competitiveness (Tiffany Melvin), BNSF (Lindsay Mullins), Killam Development Ltd. (Rolando Ortiz), Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (Steven Schar), Union Pacific Railroad (Clint Schelbitzki), Port of Victoria (Sean Stibich), McAllen Economic Development Corporation (Isaac Tawil), Kirby Corporation (Matt Woodruff), and Ex-Officio: Judge Ed Emmett, Rice University. *Source: Texas Freight Advisory Committee, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/about/advisory-committees/texas-freight-advisory-committee.html

*Analytical inference:* The TxFAC membership composition signals TxDOT's freight planning priorities: the inclusion of BNSF and Union Pacific alongside Port Houston, Port of Victoria, Ports to Plains Alliance, and the McAllen and Mission EDCs reflects a multimodal orientation weighted toward rail, maritime, and border-crossing corridors. Workspaces operating in those corridors have direct representation-adjacent access through TxFAC's engagement channels.

**U.S. DOT as federal framework setter:**

FACT — Federal law "requires USDOT to periodically update and implement an NFSP." The 2026 NFSP "was developed through a multi-agency effort that includes extensive consultation with freight stakeholders and national experts." *Source: National Freight Strategic Plan (NFSP), U.S. DOT, updated May 18, 2026. https://www.transportation.gov/freight/NFSP

**FHWA as capacity-building resource:**

FACT — The FHWA Freight Professional Capacity Building Program operates a State Freight Planning Toolkit described as "A resource hub for freight transportation planning" covering seven key factors including "Empower Advisory Committees," "Leverage All Data," "Coordinate and Collaborate," "Connect to Other Plans and Programs," "Identify and Fund Freight Projects," "Market Freight Plans," and "Measure, Monitor, Evaluate." *Source: FHWA Freight Professional Capacity Building Program, Federal Highway Administration. https://ops.fhwa.dot.gov/freight/fpcb/toolkit/default.aspx

# Expected Trajectory and Conversion Triggers

**Confirmed phased timeline (FACT):**

TxDOT has published the following development schedule for the 2027 TFMP:

- **Late 2025 through Late 2027** — Public involvement active
- **Late 2025** — Project kickoff, data assessment, analysis of trends, disruptors, and opportunities
- **Early 2026** — Multimodal data inventory, conduct a data needs assessment, supply chain review, critical freight facility assessment
- **Mid 2026** — Begin stakeholder engagement, conduct a freight bottleneck and network assessments
- **Late 2026** — Document economic importance of multimodal freight, finalize Texas freight mobility recommendations, finalize implementation plan

*Source: TxDOT Texas Freight Mobility Plan page, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/projects/planning/freight-planning/texas-freight-mobility-plan.html

*Analytical inference:* The mid-2026 stakeholder engagement phase is the critical access window for freight operators and intermediaries. The late-2026 recommendation finalization phase is when the plan's project and funding priorities solidify. Workspaces that engage before mid-2026 have the greatest opportunity to shape bottleneck identification and corridor prioritization. After late 2026, the plan moves to publication rather than input.

**TxFAC 2026 meeting schedule (FACT):**

- March 26, 2026 — Dewitt C Greer State Highway Building, Austin
- June 8, 2026 — TxDOT Stassney Campus, Austin
- September 9, 2026 — TBD
- December 3, 2026 — TBD

*Source: Texas Freight Advisory Committee, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/about/advisory-committees/texas-freight-advisory-committee.html

**Conversion trigger:** The 2027 TFMP moves from signal to binding infrastructure investment when TxDOT adopts the final plan (scheduled late 2027). Projects and corridors named in the final plan flow into TxDOT's Unified Transportation Program (UTP) and competitive federal funding applications. Corridors and bottlenecks NOT named are deprioritized for state capital investment in the next four-year cycle.

*Analytical inference:* The September and December 2026 TxFAC meetings — both with TBD locations — are likely the sessions at which draft recommendations are reviewed before finalization. These represent the final substantive input opportunity before the plan hardens.

# Operational and Cost Implications If It Materializes

**For workspaces moving air-priority cargo through Texas:**

*Analytical inference:* Texas hosts major air cargo hubs (DFW, IAH, SAT). If the 2027 TFMP identifies specific airport freight infrastructure bottlenecks — landside access, air cargo apron capacity, intermodal connectivity — and these flow into TxDOT capital programming, workspaces handling time-sensitive cargo on U.S. domestic and trans-border lanes face improved or degraded conditions depending on whether their hub airports receive investment priority. Effect on air-primary verticals (live events, film and TV, humanitarian, fine art) requires verification against final TFMP project list.

**For workspaces moving road-primary cargo through Texas corridors:**

FACT — Texas "has been the #1 U.S. exporter for 23 years in a row, and it is the largest energy-producing state in the Nation." *Source: TxDOT Texas Freight Mobility Plan page, TxDOT, 2026. https://www.txdot.gov/projects/planning/freight-planning/texas-freight-mobility-plan.html

*Analytical inference:* The freight bottleneck assessment scheduled for mid-2026 will identify specific road segments and border crossings under capacity pressure. Workspaces routing truckload and LTL freight through Texas border crossings (Laredo, El Paso, McAllen — all represented on TxFAC) may see those corridors either receive investment priority or be identified as systemic constraints requiring mitigation. Either outcome affects drayage availability, crossing wait times, and transit reliability on USMCA trade lanes.

**For workspaces with ocean or port exposure:**

*Analytical inference:* TxFAC includes Port Houston and Port of Victoria representatives. The TFMP's critical freight facility assessment (early 2026) will evaluate port connectivity. Workspaces routing ocean containers through Texas Gulf ports that connect to inland distribution via road or rail will have those connectivity gaps — or assets — reflected in the plan's investment recommendations.

**Cost implication:** *Specific cost impact figures not available from primary sources as of 2026-06-10.* Cost implications to the workspace are downstream of which projects receive funding, which is determined post-plan adoption. The planning process itself creates no direct cost; the risk is opportunity cost from non-participation during the input window.

# Competitive Implications

*Analytical inference:* Freight operators and intermediaries that actively participate in TxDOT's stakeholder engagement process — through TxFAC channels, MPO freight advisory committees, or direct workshop participation — gain three structural advantages: early sight of which corridors TxDOT classifies as bottlenecks (operational intelligence), early sight of which projects enter the capital pipeline (infrastructure investment lead time), and standing to advocate for corridor-specific improvements that serve their client base.

*Analytical inference:* The TxFAC membership list, as sourced, does not include any major third-party logistics provider (3PL) or international freight forwarder by name. This is a gap that workspaces in the freight forwarding intermediary role could potentially fill through direct engagement with TxDOT's freight planning office (TxDOT_Freight@txdot.gov) or by attending public TxFAC meetings as observers.

FACT — The FHWA State Freight Planning Toolkit explicitly lists "Market Freight Plans" as one of the seven key planning factors, alongside stakeholder advisory committee empowerment and freight project identification. *Source: FHWA Freight Professional Capacity Building Program, Federal Highway Administration. https://ops.fhwa.dot.gov/freight/fpcb/toolkit/default.aspx

*Analytical inference:* This toolkit structure means TxDOT has federal backing and methodological guidance for all seven planning dimensions, including how to market the completed plan to freight industry participants. Workspaces that engage now are better positioned to leverage the plan's findings in client conversations once it is published.

# Client Conversation Talking Points

**What the workspace can credibly say:**

- Texas is conducting a formal, legislatively required update of its statewide freight plan through late 2027, with stakeholder input windows open now through mid-2026.
- The plan will produce specific project, program, and policy recommendations covering all modes — road, rail, air, maritime — with a supply chain lens and a border trade focus.
- The Texas Freight Advisory Committee, which advises TxDOT on freight priorities and investment, includes representatives from BNSF, Union Pacific, Port Houston, Ports to Plains Alliance, and the McAllen and Mission economic development corporations — signaling where TxDOT sees freight system criticality.
- The 2026 U.S. DOT National Freight Strategic Plan sets the federal framework within which the Texas plan operates, covering safety, efficiency, security, resilience, innovation, and workforce across all modes.

**What the workspace should NOT claim:**

- Do not claim that specific corridors, projects, or funding amounts have been committed — the plan is still in development and recommendations are not final until late 2026 at earliest.
- Do not represent TxFAC membership as endorsement of any specific freight operator's positioning.
- Do not cite the February 2026 Federal Register guidance as having specific content in client conversations — the source was inaccessible and its provisions cannot be confirmed.

**Useful client question to pose:** "Has your organization's corridor or supply chain been included in TxDOT's bottleneck and critical facility assessments? That assessment is underway now and directly shapes the four-year capital investment pipeline."
